Mindful Growth

Guided Journey to Achievement

A 9-day international training course giving youth workers practical, research-backed tools for focus, resilience, and self-care — built on the experiential learning cycle, with daily reflection as its spine.

01

Overview

Across Europe, youth workers are burning out. Under the EU Youth Strategy, a significant share report high stress driven by workload and thin support systems. Seven organisations — from Latvia, Czechia, Hungary, Bulgaria, Georgia, Spain, and Lithuania — had independently identified the same pattern: capable, committed people whose effectiveness was being quietly eroded by disorganisation, digital overload, and exhaustion.

Mindful Growth was designed to address this directly — not through motivational talks, but through a practical, experiential programme that equips youth workers with tools they can use the week they return home. The programme was approved for €60,000 in Erasmus+ funding.

02

Challenge

Self-care and resilience are easy topics to fill with platitudes. The audience — professional facilitators who run wellbeing sessions for a living — can identify a hollow experience quickly.

Design something practical enough to survive a room full of sceptical professionals, transferable enough to work across seven national contexts, and personal enough to change individual habits — all in nine days, mostly without slides.

A lecture series would have been the easy build and a guaranteed failure. The design had to be experiential from the ground up.

03

Target Audience

30 youth workers, trainers, facilitators, and active volunteers aged 22–30, drawn from 7 partner organisations across Europe. Deliberately diverse — including participants facing economic, geographic, or social barriers — they reach young people through sport, creative workshops, community hubs, mentoring, and outdoor programmes.

Common challenge
Multi-role, high-load work in under-resourced settings where personal wellbeing is the first thing dropped
Key gaps identified
Time management · digital distraction · habit-building · focus & resilience
Design constraints
9 days · non-formal methods · multicultural · mixed prior experience

Before arrival, each participant completes a structured pre-course reflection on their current routines and biggest challenges. This primes self-awareness and gives trainers a real read on the group so sessions can be tuned to the actual people in the room.

04

Learning Objectives

Written as observable shifts — things you could watch a person start doing differently — each tied to a Youthpass competence.

  • Plan a realistic week and protect the time they set aside, using a repeatable planning routine.
  • Notice their own stress and digital-overload patterns early enough to act on them.
  • Practise reflection as a daily habit through journaling — not just as an end-of-session event.
  • Design one concrete tool — a planner, a routine, a reflection prompt — they can use the week they return home.
  • Adapt these methods to their own cultural and organisational context, and pass them on to their teams.

06

Design Process

The whole design sits on Kolb's experiential learning cycle: a concrete experience, structured reflection, a concept to name what happened, then a chance to try it differently. Every session moves through that loop at least once — turning an activity into a transferable insight rather than a well-designed hour.

The content draws on behaviour-change research — BJ Fogg and James Clear on small, consistent habits — and on Moon's reflective practice framework for the journaling spine. The core bet: durable change comes from tiny repeated actions plus honest reflection, not inspiration that fades by Tuesday.

Alternative considered and rejected

An expert-led curriculum of talks on stress and productivity would have been faster to build. I rejected it because the audience are experts themselves, and lecture content doesn't transfer across cultures or survive a busy week back home. Experiential design costs more to build. It was the only approach that could actually work here.

07

Workshop Structure

Nine days built as a journey, not a syllabus — moving from understanding yourself early, toward real-world practice and peer exchange later. Each day pairs a planning or focus skill with an emotional-awareness thread, and closes with structured reflection.

Days 1–3

Arrive & Notice

Journaling starter, group agreements, self-observation. Participants surface their own patterns before any solution appears.

→
Days 4–6

Practise the Tools

Hands-on experiments — focus drills, habit-building, planning routines, "Social Media Mood Swing" and "Disconnect to Reconnect" activities — each followed by structured reflection.

→
Days 7–9

Make It Transferable

Peer exchange across countries, collaborative mini-projects, and a personal follow-up plan: one concrete tool each participant will use back home.

Reflection runs at three scales: a daily written pause, a structured debrief after each activity that completes the Kolb loop, and a running journal with Youthpass-aligned self-assessment across the full nine days.

Expected Outcomes

Intended results — stated as design targets, because the programme runs in August 2026:

30
youth workers equipped with practical planning, focus, and resilience tools.
1+
concrete personal tool designed and tested by each participant to use back home.
7
partner organisations with reusable methods for sustainable, lower-burnout practice.
Self

Structured self-assessment before, during, and after — mapped to Youthpass competences so learners can see and name their own growth.

Group

Daily reflection and facilitator observation feed continuous adjustment during the course, not just a verdict at the end.

Transfer

Follow-up checks whether people applied a tool back home. Target: most participants run at least one local follow-up activity.

09

Reflection

Designing this stretched me in a specific way: building something rigorous on a subject that invites platitudes. The discipline that saved it was refusing to write a single objective I couldn't picture someone doing — then defending every activity against that standard.

It also changed how I think about reflection in my work generally. I'd always included it; designing this made me treat it as the primary mechanism rather than a closing courtesy. The experience is the raw material. Reflection is what turns it into a behaviour someone actually keeps.

Working across seven national contexts taught me to build for adaptation, not control. The best thing I could hand these facilitators wasn't a fixed programme — it was a set of tools flexible enough to survive translation into their own rooms, with their own participants, long after I was gone.

If the only place a design works is the room you built it in, you haven't finished designing. The real test is whether it travels home.
