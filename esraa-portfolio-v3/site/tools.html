Tools

The toolkit, grouped by what I use it for.

Tools are means, not the point — but knowing which one fits which job is part of the craft. Here's mine, and how I actually use each.

Learning design & build

Turning a design into something learners can actually use.

Articulate Storyline 360Interactive e-learning and scenario-based modules — branching, knowledge checks, the things that make digital learning active rather than a slideshow.
PowerPointFacilitation decks and visual scaffolding for live sessions — used as a support for the room, never as the lesson itself.
CanvaFast, clean learner materials, worksheets, and visual aids — especially when a session needs handouts that look considered, not photocopied.

Design & UX

The user-centred craft that informs how I design learning.

FigmaUI design, wireframing, and prototyping — and increasingly, mapping learning journeys the way I'd map a product flow.
FigJamCollaborative mapping with stakeholders and SMEs — needs analysis, activity maps, and journey diagrams done in the open.
PhotoshopVisual asset production and refinement when a project needs more than a template can give.

AI & productivity

Used deliberately — to move faster on drafts, never to replace the thinking.

ChatGPTFirst drafts, scenario variations, and pressure-testing objectives — a thinking partner for the parts that benefit from speed.
Notion AIOrganising research, documentation, and programme planning so the structure stays visible.
Leonardo AIGenerating custom visuals and imagery for learning materials when stock won't do.
Microsoft OfficeThe everyday backbone — documents, data, and the unglamorous coordination every real programme runs on.

How I think about tools: the design decides the tool, never the other way around. I'd rather run a powerful session with paper and a pen — which is exactly what the best workshops in my library use — than build something slick that doesn't change anyone's behaviour.

Designing learning experiences that create engagement, reflection & behaviour change.
