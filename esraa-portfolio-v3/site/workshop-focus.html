Workshop library Workshop 02

Focus, Attention & Social Media

The app on trial.

A session that makes an invisible habit visible. Instead of telling people social media affects their focus, it gets them to prove it to themselves — with their own numbers, their own mood data, and a role-play they don't forget.

Purpose
Build honest awareness of digital habits
Audience
Youth workers & volunteers
Core method
Self-generated evidence · role-play
Ends with
A symbolic commitment & real tools

The behaviour I wanted to shift

Everyone "knows" they spend too long on their phone. Knowing changes nothing. The gap between abstract awareness and felt awareness is exactly where this session works. I didn't want to lecture people about screen time — I wanted to make them feel the cost in a way a statistic never could.

Learning objectives

  • Confront their real usage data and what it adds up to over a month and a year.
  • Locate agency — recognising that the app is designed to pull attention, but the choice stays theirs.
  • Connect constant scrolling to their own emotional volatility and loss of focus.
  • Choose concrete alternatives for their time and attention.

How the session runs

  1. 1

    The numbers, on their own screens

    Everyone opens their own screen-time report and writes down yesterday's figure, then their weekly average. Then we do the arithmetic together — per month, per year. I give the room a few seconds of silence to sit with it. The evidence is theirs, so it can't be argued with.

  2. 2

    "The App on Trial" — a role-play

    In pairs, one person plays themselves, blaming the app for stealing their time. The other plays the app, whose only line is some version of "I didn't force you." Then they switch. It's playful, but it does serious work: it holds two truths at once — the app is engineered to capture attention, and the final control still belongs to the user. That second truth is what makes change possible.

  3. 3

    "Social Media Mood Swing"

    I play a sequence of short videos with deliberately mixed emotions — funny, sad, boring, overstimulating — back to back, simulating a real scroll. After each one, participants rate their mood 1–10 and write it down. At the end, they connect the dots into a line. The jagged graph that appears is the point: this is what your attention and emotions are doing dozens of times a day. We talk about why focus is so hard to recover after that.

  4. 4

    Name it, then tear it up

    Each person writes down their single biggest time-wasting habit — then physically tears the paper, as a symbolic decision not to return to it. It's small and a little theatrical, and it works: a felt commitment beats a mental one.

  5. 5

    Replace, don't just remove

    We close on what they actually want to do with the reclaimed time and attention, plus practical tools for controlling usage, reducing distraction, and protecting focus. Removing a habit without a replacement just leaves a vacuum the old habit fills back up.

Why it's built this way

The whole session is one principle applied repeatedly: people believe what they discover, not what they're told. I never assert that phones harm focus. I design the conditions for participants to calculate it, role-play it, and graph it themselves. By the time we reach commitment, the case has already been made — by them, to them. That's why it sticks where a slide deck of statistics wouldn't.

Reflection & outcome

Participants leave with a felt, personal understanding of how their digital habits shape their focus and mood — and a concrete first alternative to reach for instead. The mood-swing graph in particular tends to be the thing people mention afterwards; seeing your own emotional volatility drawn as a line is hard to un-see.

Facilitator's note

Timing the video sequence is everything — too short and the effect doesn't build, too long and people disengage. And the silence after the screen-time arithmetic has to be protected. The instinct is to fill it. Don't. The discomfort is the lesson.

What I'd change next time

I'd add an optional follow-up where people re-check their screen time a week later — the session is strong at creating the moment of insight but, like a lot of single sessions, leans on the participant to sustain it. Building in one lightweight check-in would turn a powerful afternoon into a measurable behaviour change.
